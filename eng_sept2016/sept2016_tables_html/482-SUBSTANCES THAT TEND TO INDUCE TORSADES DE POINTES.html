TABLE OF INTERACTIONS WITH

SUBSTANCES THAT TEND TO INDUCE TORSADES DE POINTES

This serious disorder of cardiac rhythm can be provoked by a certain number of medications, antiarrhythmics or not. Hypokaliemia (cf. Potassium Lowering Medications) is a conducive factor, as well as bradycardia (cf. Bradycardia Inducing Drugs) or a pre-existing lengthening of the QT interval, congenital or acquired.

The medications at the origin of this undesirable effect are especially the antiarrhythmics of class 1a and 111, and certain neuroleptics. Other molecules not belonging to these classes are also implicated.

For the erythromycin and the vincamine, only the forms administered by intravenous route are concerned by this interaction.

The use of a medication that induces torsades de pointes with another medication that does is contraindicated as a general rule.

Even so, some of them, by reason of their unavoidable character, are exceptions to the rule, being only not recommended with the other medications that induce torsades de pointes. It is a matter of methodone, hydroxychloroquin, the antiparasitics (chloroquin, halofantrine, lumefantrine, pentamidine) and the neuroleptics.

However, citalopram, escitalopram, domperidone, hydroxyzine and piperaquin are not included in this exception and are contraindicated with all the medications that induce torsades de pointes.
